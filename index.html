Projects

High-throughput methods for quantifying neuroanatomy

I am actively developing methods for high-throughput neuroanatomy that utilize both X-ray microtomography and electron microscopy (EM). Our main aim is to use X-rays to perform mesoscale (1 micron) analyses on brain volumes without sectioning. This technique provides high data rates, high resolution (at mesoscale), and volumes that provides dense labeling of cell bodies, vessels, and large neurites (myelinated axons and apical dendrites). After X-ray imaging, we then apply serial EM to the same samples to obtain nanoscale (connectomics) information.

This is highly multi-disciplinary collaboration between researchers at Northwestern, University of Chicago, and Argonne National Laboratory.

Related publications:

Low-dimensional signal models

Unions of subspaces are a powerful generalization of single subspace models, which model data points as living on multiple subspaces, rather than assuming a global low-dimensional model. Modeling data with mixtures of subspaces provides a more compact and simple representation of the data. In these cases, finding multiple subspaces, or subspace clustering, can provide a better partitioning (clustering) of the data or also help to denoise the data.

Related publications:

Methods for analyzing functional neural data

I am currently developing methods for brain decoding that exploit low-dimensional structure in neural data to learn a decoder of brain activity without known correspondence between the input and outputs to the system. In the past, I also worked on methods for decoding spikes from neuronal calcium time-series.
